# Hepatitis C has no vaccine because its RNA-dependent RNA polymerase cannot proofread, generating a cloud of quasi-species variants that outpace immune recognition

The HCV replication complex uses an RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRP) to copy its genome. Unlike DNA polymerases, RdRP lacks 3' to 5' exonuclease proofreading activity. Errors made during replication are not corrected.

Because replication is error-prone and the virus replicates rapidly, an infected patient carries not a single viral sequence but a swarm of closely related variants. This is called a quasi-species. Each variant differs slightly in its envelope proteins, the targets that antibodies would need to recognize.

The immune system is always chasing yesterday's variants. By the time the host mounts an antibody response to one variant sequence, the population has already drifted. New mutant strains emerge continuously, and the immune system perpetually lags. The result: no individual develops a complete, protective antibody response, and designing a single vaccine antigen that covers the range of circulating variants is extremely difficult.

This mechanism contrasts with HBV, where the virus is a DNA virus with better replication fidelity, and a surface antigen (HBsAg) that is conserved enough to serve as an effective vaccine target.

The principle generalizes to other RNA viruses: influenza, HIV, and SARS-CoV-2 all exploit polymerase infidelity to escape immune surveillance. HCV is the canonical illustration of why vaccine design against RNA viruses is structurally harder than against DNA viruses.

# Cancer requires sequential accumulation of 6-7 driver mutations, not a single event

No single mutation causes cancer in an otherwise normal cell. The adenoma-carcinoma sequence in colorectal cancer is the best-characterised model: APC loss creates a polyp, KRAS activation drives growth, SMAD4 and TP53 loss remove remaining checkpoints, and additional hits allow invasion and metastasis. At least six to seven sequential alterations are required.

This explains cancer's long latency (decades of accumulation before a clinical tumor), why familial cancer syndromes accelerate development (one hit inherited means fewer somatic hits needed), and why a single activated oncogene like H-ras in normal cells causes only hyperplasia, not neoplasia.

Each mutation provides a selective growth advantage, creating clonal expansion that increases the probability of the next hit. Cancer is Darwinian evolution at the cellular level: natural selection acting on somatic mutations.

The implication for screening: if you can detect pre-cancerous lesions with fewer than the full complement of driver mutations (adenomatous polyps in FAP), removing them before the remaining hits accumulate is curative. This is why colonoscopy reduces colorectal cancer mortality.

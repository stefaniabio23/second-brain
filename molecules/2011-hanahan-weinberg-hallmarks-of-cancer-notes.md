# Hallmarks of Cancer: The Next Generation (Hanahan & Weinberg 2011)

The 2011 sequel to Hanahan and Weinberg's 2000 *Hallmarks of Cancer*, published in *Cell* 144 (March 4, 2011), is a framework paper, not a primary-research paper. It revisits the six original hallmarks, adds two emerging hallmarks and two enabling characteristics, and reframes tumour biology around a tumour-organ view rather than a cancer-cell-autonomous view. For an analyst, this is the canonical reference grid for mapping any oncology drug to the capability it disrupts.

## The six original hallmarks

The 2000 paper proposed six acquired capabilities that, together, distinguish cancer from normal tissue. The 2011 update keeps all six but rewords each toward mechanism.

1. **Sustaining proliferative signalling.** Cancer cells produce growth-factor ligands themselves (autocrine), signal to stroma which signals back (paracrine), overexpress receptors, or activate downstream pathways directly. ~40% of human melanomas carry activating B-Raf mutations triggering constitutive Ras-MAPK signalling; PI3K catalytic-subunit mutations hyperactivate Akt/PKB across multiple tumour types.
2. **Evading growth suppressors.** The two prototype tumour suppressors, RB and TP53, govern proliferate-versus-arrest and survive-versus-die decisions. Contact inhibition, mediated by NF2/Merlin and LKB1, is abrogated. TGF-β switches in late-stage tumours from anti-proliferative signal to EMT driver.
3. **Resisting cell death.** TP53 loss removes the central damage-sensor arm of apoptosis. Cancer cells also upregulate antiapoptotic regulators (Bcl-2, Bcl-xL), downregulate proapoptotic factors (Bax, Bim, Puma), or short-circuit extrinsic death-receptor signalling. Autophagy and necrosis enter the picture: autophagy is dual-use (barrier early, survival mechanism late), and necrotic debris recruits tumour-promoting inflammatory cells.
4. **Enabling replicative immortality.** ~90% of immortalised and human cancer cells express telomerase at functionally significant levels, countering the telomere erosion that triggers senescence or crisis. Telomerase/TERT also has non-canonical functions: cofactor for the Wnt/β-catenin transcription complex, DNA-damage-response modulator, RNA-dependent RNA polymerase activity.
5. **Inducing angiogenesis.** The angiogenic switch flips early in multistep tumorigenesis, often in premalignant lesions. VEGF-A and FGF family ligands drive it; thrombospondin-1 opposes it. Tumour vasculature is aberrant: precocious sprouting, leakiness, microhemorrhaging, erratic flow. Pericyte coverage is loose but present, and matters for vascular integrity.
6. **Activating invasion and metastasis.** Loss of E-cadherin is the best-characterised molecular event. The EMT program (epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition), orchestrated by Snail, Slug, Twist and Zeb1/2, programs invasiveness, motility, and apoptosis-resistance. The invasion-metastasis cascade is sequenced as local invasion → intravasation → transit → extravasation → micrometastasis → colonisation, with colonisation as the rate-limiting step. Many disseminated tumour cells persist as dormant micrometastases that never form macroscopic disease.

## The two emerging hallmarks (new in 2011)

7. **Deregulating cellular energetics ("aerobic glycolysis" / Warburg effect).** Cancer cells reprogram glucose metabolism toward glycolysis with lactate production even under normoxia, despite an ~18-fold lower ATP yield than oxidative phosphorylation. Compensated by upregulating GLUT1 glucose transporters, which is exploited diagnostically by FDG-PET. RAS, MYC and TP53 loss drive the switch; HIF1α and HIF2α amplify it under hypoxia. Some tumours show intratumoural symbiosis: hypoxic cells secrete lactate consumed by oxygenated neighbours.

8. **Avoiding immune destruction.** Engineered-mouse and clinical-epidemiology evidence indicates the immune system (CD8+ CTLs, CD4+ Th1, NK cells) actively suppresses tumour initiation and progression in non-virus-associated cancers. Tumours that escape do so by "immunoediting": deletion of immunogenic clones, paralysis of CTLs and NKs via TGF-β secretion, and recruitment of Tregs and MDSCs (myeloid-derived suppressor cells).

## The two enabling characteristics

These two are not hallmarks themselves but make hallmarks acquirable.

- **Genome instability and mutation.** Defects in the DNA-maintenance "caretaker" machinery (detection, repair, mutagen interception) raise mutation rates. TP53 loss removes the gate that would otherwise force damaged cells into senescence or apoptosis. Telomerase shortage during incipient progression generates breakage-fusion-bridge cycles and additional karyotypic instability.
- **Tumour-promoting inflammation.** Effectively every neoplastic lesion contains immune-cell infiltrate at some density. Inflammatory cells supply growth factors, survival factors, proangiogenic factors, EMT-inducing signals, and reactive oxygen species that are mutagenic for neighbouring cancer cells. Inflammation operates as enabler for multiple hallmark capabilities at once.

## Tumour as organ, not lump

The paper's biggest reframe is the tumour microenvironment. A tumour is a multicellular tissue with parenchyma (cancer cells, including cancer stem cells) and stroma (cancer-associated fibroblasts, endothelial cells, pericytes, immune-inflammatory cells, mesenchymal stem cells, bone-marrow-derived progenitors). Heterotypic signalling between these compartments orchestrates hallmark acquisition: macrophages supply EGF to breast cancer cells, breast cancer cells supply CSF-1 back; mesenchymal stem cells secrete CCL5 that drives metastatic behaviour. Microenvironments evolve across primary, invasive and metastatic stages. "Metastatic niches" can preexist at distant sites or be conditioned at a distance by primary-tumour factors.

Cancer stem cells (CSCs) are a phenotypic subpopulation defined operationally by xenograft-initiating ability. They share transcriptional profiles with tissue stem cells, are linked to EMT (induction of EMT in epithelial cells produces stem-cell traits), and resist conventional chemo/radio. CSC persistence after debulking is implicated in dormancy and relapse.

## Therapeutic implication: hallmark drugs and adaptive resistance

The paper closes by mapping hallmark capabilities to drug classes: EGFR inhibitors → proliferative signalling; CDK inhibitors → growth-suppressor evasion; BH3 mimetics → apoptosis resistance; telomerase inhibitors → replicative immortality; VEGF inhibitors → angiogenesis; HGF/c-Met inhibitors → invasion/metastasis; aerobic-glycolysis inhibitors → energetics; immune-activating anti-CTLA4 mAb → immune evasion; selective anti-inflammatories → tumour-promoting inflammation; PARP inhibitors → genome instability. Clinical responses to single-hallmark drugs have been transitory, followed by relapse, because tumours adapt by activating redundant pathways within the same hallmark or by shifting dependence to a different hallmark. The strategic implication: durable therapy needs combination cotargeting of multiple hallmarks plus enabling characteristics.

## Why this matters for an investor

The hallmarks are the working ontology for oncology drug development. Any oncology asset can be located on this grid by mechanism of action, which gives a fast read on competitive intensity within a hallmark, on probability of combination utility, and on resistance trajectory. The 2011 update is also where energetics and immune evasion become first-class targets, foreshadowing checkpoint inhibitors and metabolic-oncology platforms.
